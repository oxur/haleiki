**Gandhara** ([Sanskrit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sanskrit_language "Sanskrit language"): गन्धारः, [romanized](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Romanization_of_Sanskrit "Romanization of Sanskrit"): _Gandhāra_) was an ancient [Indo-Aryan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indo-Aryan_people "Indo-Aryan people") civilisation located in present-day northwestern [Pakistan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pakistan "Pakistan") and northeastern [Afghanistan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Afghanistan "Afghanistan"). The core of the region of Gandhara was the [Valley of Peshawar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Valley_of_Peshawar "Valley of Peshawar"), though the cultural influence of "Greater Gandhara" extended across the [Indus River](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indus_River "Indus River") to [Taxila](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taxila "Taxila") and westwards into the [Kabul Valley](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kabul_Valley "Kabul Valley") as far as [Bamyan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bamyan "Bamyan"), and northwards up to the [Karakoram](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Karakoram "Karakoram") range, including [Swat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Swat_District "Swat District"), [Bajaur](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bajaur_District "Bajaur District") and other valleys.

The _Gandhara tribe_, after which it is named, is attested in the [Rigveda](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rigveda "Rigveda") (c. 1500 – c. 1200 BCE), while the region is mentioned in the Zoroastrian [Avesta](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Avesta "Avesta") as _Vaēkərəta_, the [seventh most beautiful place](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Avestan_geography "Avestan geography") on earth created by [Ahura Mazda](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ahura_Mazda "Ahura Mazda"). Gandhara was one of the 16 _[Great Realms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mahajanapadas "Mahajanapadas")_ of the [second urbanisation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_India#Second_urbanisation_\(c._600_–_200_BCE\) "History of India").

The [Iron Age](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iron_Age_in_India "Iron Age in India") [Gandhara kingdom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gandhāra_\(kingdom\) "Gandhāra (kingdom)") was [conquered by Cyrus I](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Achaemenid_conquest_of_the_Indus_Valley "Achaemenid conquest of the Indus Valley") in the 6th century BCE who incorporated it as the satrapy of [Gandāra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gandāra "Gandāra") into the [Achaemenid Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Achaemenid_Empire "Achaemenid Empire"), later [being conquered](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cophen_campaign "Cophen campaign") by [Alexander the Great](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alexander_the_Great "Alexander the Great") in 327 BCE. Afterwards it became a part of the [Maurya Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maurya_Empire "Maurya Empire"), followed by the [Indo-Greek Kingdom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indo-Greek_Kingdom "Indo-Greek Kingdom"). The region was a major centre for [Greco-Buddhism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greco-Buddhism "Greco-Buddhism") under the Indo-Greeks and [Gandharan Buddhism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gandharan_Buddhism "Gandharan Buddhism") under later dynasties; Gandhara was also the central location for the [Silk Road transmission of Buddhism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silk_Road_transmission_of_Buddhism "Silk Road transmission of Buddhism") to Central and East Asia.

[Gandhārī](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gandhari_language "Gandhari language"), an [Indo-Aryan language](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indo-Aryan_languages "Indo-Aryan languages") written in [Kharosthi script](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kharosthi_script "Kharosthi script"), acted as lingua franca of the region. Famed for its unique [Gandharan style of art](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gandhara_art "Gandhara art") influenced by the classical Hellenistic styles, Gandhara attained its height from the 1st century to the 5th century CE under the [Kushan Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kushan_Empire "Kushan Empire"), who had their capital at Peshawar (_[Puruṣapura](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Puruṣapura "Puruṣapura")_) and ushered a period of relative peace known as _[Pax Kushana](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pax_Kushana "Pax Kushana")_. Gandhara "flourished at the crossroads of India, Central Asia, and the Middle East," connecting [trade routes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silk_Road "Silk Road") and absorbing cultural influences from diverse civilizations; Buddhism thrived until the 8th or 9th centuries, when [Islam](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Islam "Islam") first began to gain sway in the region.

The region declined after the invasion by the [Alchon Huns](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alchon_Huns "Alchon Huns") in 6th century, and the name Gandhara disappeared after [Mahmud Ghaznavi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mahmud_Ghaznavi "Mahmud Ghaznavi")'s conquest of [Hindu Shahi Kingdom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hindu_Shahis "Hindu Shahis") in 1001 CE.

## Etymology

Gandhara was known in [Sanskrit](/source/sanskrit/ "Sanskrit") as Gandhāraḥ ([गन्धारः](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/गन्धार "wikt:गन्धार")) and in [Avestan](/source/avestan/ "Avestan") as '_Vaēkərəta_. In [Old Persian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Old_Persian "Old Persian"), Gandhara was known as [Gadāra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gadāra "Gadāra") ([𐎥𐎭𐎠𐎼](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/𐎥𐎭𐎠𐎼 "wikt:𐎥𐎭𐎠𐎼"), also transliterated as Gandāra since the nasal "n" before consonants were omitted in Old Persian). In [Chinese](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chinese_language "Chinese language"), Gandhara is known as _Jiāntuóluó_ ([traditional Chinese](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Traditional_Chinese_characters "Traditional Chinese characters"):[犍陀羅](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/犍陀羅#Chinese "wikt:犍陀羅"); [simplified Chinese](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Simplified_Chinese_characters "Simplified Chinese characters"):犍陀罗, also written [健馱邏](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/健馱邏#Chinese "wikt:健馱邏"); 健驮逻), with the Middle Chinese pronunciation reconstructed as _kɨɐndala_. One state of the region named _[Jìbīn](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jibin "Jibin")_ ([罽賓](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/罽賓#Chinese "wikt:罽賓"); 罽宾, also romanised as Kipin) is recorded in the [Book of Han](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Book_of_Han "Book of Han").

One proposed origin of the name is from the Sanskrit word __gandhaḥ__ ([गन्धः](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/गन्ध "wikt:गन्ध")), meaning "perfume" and "referring to the spices and aromatic herbs which they (the inhabitants) traded and with which they anointed themselves". The [Gandhari people](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gandhari_people "Gandhari people") are a [tribe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_Rigvedic_tribes "List of Rigvedic tribes") mentioned in the [Rigveda](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rigveda "Rigveda"), the [Atharvaveda](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atharvaveda "Atharvaveda"), and later Vedic texts. This origin is also supported by the word _Gandhara_ meaning “fragrance bringer” in the [Pashayi language](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pashayi_languages "Pashayi languages"). A [Persian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Persian_language "Persian language") form of the name, _Gandara_, mentioned in the [Behistun inscription](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Behistun_inscription "Behistun inscription") of Emperor [Darius I](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Darius_I "Darius I"), was translated as _[Paruparaesanna](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paruparaesanna "Paruparaesanna")_ (__Para-upari-sena__, meaning "beyond the Hindu Kush") in [Babylonian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Babylonian_language "Babylonian language") and [Elamite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elamite_language "Elamite language") in the same inscription. In [Greek](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_Greek "Ancient Greek"), Gandhara was known as [Paropamisadae](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paropamisadae "Paropamisadae")**.**

## Geography

The geographical extent of Gandhara underwent much alteration throughout history, with the core being the region bounded by the Indus River, the Swat Valley, and the [Kabul River](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kabul_River "Kabul River"). Its prominent urban centres were [Taxila](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taxila "Taxila") and [Pushkalavati](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pushkalavati "Pushkalavati"). The cultural influence of Gandhara stretched as far as [Jhelum River](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jhelum_River "Jhelum River") in the east as evident by the discovery of Gandhara style Buddhist artworks there. One [Jataka](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jataka_tales "Jataka tales") even includes [Kashmir](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kashmir "Kashmir") within Gandhara.

[Richard Solomon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Richard_G._Salomon_\(professor_of_Asian_studies\) "Richard G. Salomon (professor of Asian studies)") uses the term "Greater Gandhara" to describe the extent of cultural influence of Gandhara and its language, Gandhari. In the later historical sources, Greater Gandhara encompassed the territories of [Jibin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jibin "Jibin") and [Oddiyana](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oddiyana "Oddiyana"), extending into parts of [Bactria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bactria "Bactria") and the [Tarim Basin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tarim_Basin "Tarim Basin"). Oddiyana was situated in the vicinity of the Swat Valley, while Jibin corresponded to the region of [Kapisa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kapisa_\(city\) "Kapisa (city)"), south of the Hindu Kush.

The Udichya region was noted by 6th century BCE scholar [Pāṇini](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pāṇini "Pāṇini") as comprising both the regions of [Vahika](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vahika "Vahika") and Gandhara.

## History

### Gandhāra grave culture

![Cremation urn, Gandhara grave culture, Swat Valley, c. 1200 BCE](../media/gandhara/Cremation_Urn_with_Lid_LACMA_AC1994.234.8a-b.jpg)Cremation urn, [Gandhara grave culture](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gandhara_grave_culture "Gandhara grave culture"), Swat Valley, c. 1200 BCE

Gandhara's first recorded culture was the Grave Culture that emerged c. 1200 BCE and lasted until 800 BCE, and named for their distinct funerary practices. It was found along the Middle [Swat River](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Swat_River "Swat River") course, even though earlier research considered it to be expanded to the Valleys of [Dir](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dir_\(region\) "Dir (region)"), [Kunar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kunar_Province "Kunar Province"), [Chitral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chitral "Chitral"), and [Peshawar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Valley_of_Peshawar "Valley of Peshawar"). It has been regarded as a token of the Indo-Aryan migrations but has also been explained by local cultural continuity. Backwards projections, based on ancient DNA analyses, suggest ancestors of Swat culture people mixed with a population coming from [Inner Asia Mountain Corridor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inner_Asia_Mountain_Corridor "Inner Asia Mountain Corridor"), which carried [Steppe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eurasian_Steppe "Eurasian Steppe") ancestry, sometime between 1900 and 1500 BCE.

### Vedic Gandhāra

![Kingdoms and cities of ancient Buddhism, with Gandhara located in the northwest of this region, during the time of the Buddha (c. 500 BCE)](../media/gandhara/Mahajanapadas_%28c._500_BCE%29.png)Kingdoms and cities of ancient Buddhism, with Gandhara located in the northwest of this region, during the time of the Buddha (c. 500 BCE)

According to [Rigvedic tradition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rigveda "Rigveda"), [Yayati](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yayati "Yayati") was the progenitor of the prominent Udichya (Gandhara and [Vahika](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vahika "Vahika") tribes) and had numerous sons, including Anu, Puru, and Druhyu. The lineage of Anu gave rise to the [Madra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Madra "Madra"), [Kekaya](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kekaya "Kekaya"), [Sivi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sivi_Kingdom "Sivi Kingdom") and [Uśīnara](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uśīnara "Uśīnara") kingdoms, while the Druhyu tribe has been associated with the Gandhara kingdom.

The first mention of the Gandhārīs is attested once in the [_Ṛigveda_](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rigveda "Rigveda") as a tribe that has sheep with good wool. In the [_Atharvaveda_](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atharvaveda "Atharvaveda"), the Gandhārīs are mentioned alongside the Mūjavants, the [Āṅgeyas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anga "Anga") and the [Māgadhīs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magadha_\(Mahajanapada\) "Magadha (Mahajanapada)") in a hymn asking fever to leave the body of the sick man and instead go those aforementioned tribes. The tribes listed were the furthermost border tribes known to those in [_Madhyadeśa_](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Madhyadesha "Madhyadesha"), the Āṅgeyas and Māgadhīs in the east, and the Mūjavants and Gandhārīs in the north. The _Gandhara tribe_, after which it is named, is attested in the [Rigveda](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rigveda "Rigveda") (c. 1500 – c. 1200 BCE), while the region is mentioned in the Zoroastrian [Avesta](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Avesta "Avesta") as _Vaēkərəta_, the [seventh most beautiful place](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Avestan_geography "Avestan geography") on earth created by [Ahura Mazda](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ahura_Mazda "Ahura Mazda").

The Gāndhārī king [Nagnajit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nagnajit "Nagnajit") and his son Svarajit are mentioned in the [_Brāhmaṇa_s](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brahmana "Brahmana"), according to which they received Brahmanic consecration, but their family's attitude towards ritual is mentioned negatively, with the royal family of Gandhāra during this period following non-Brahmanical religious traditions. According to the [Jain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jainism "Jainism") [_Uttarādhyayana-sūtra_](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uttaradhyayana "Uttaradhyayana"), Nagnajit, or Naggaji, was a prominent king who had adopted Jainism and was comparable to Dvimukha of [Pāñcāla](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pancala "Pancala"), Nimi of [Videha](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Videha "Videha"), Karakaṇḍu of [Kaliṅga](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kalinga_\(historical_region\) "Kalinga (historical region)"), and Bhīma of [Vidarbha](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vidarbha "Vidarbha"); [Buddhist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Buddhism "Buddhism") sources instead claim that he had achieved [_paccekabuddhayāna_](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pratyekabuddhayāna "Pratyekabuddhayāna").

By the later [Vedic period](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vedic_period "Vedic period"), the situation had changed, and the Gāndhārī capital of [Takṣaśila](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taxila "Taxila") had become an important centre of knowledge where the men of _Madhya-desa_ went to learn the three Vedas and the eighteen branches of knowledge, with the _Kauśītaki Brāhmaṇa_ recording that [_brāhmaṇa_s](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brahmin "Brahmin") went north to study. According to the [_Śatapatha Brāhmaṇa_](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shatapatha_Brahmana "Shatapatha Brahmana") and the _Uddālaka Jātaka_, the famous Vedic philosopher [Uddālaka Āruṇi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uddālaka_Āruṇi "Uddālaka Āruṇi") was among the famous students of Takṣaśila, and the _Setaketu Jātaka_ claims that his son Śvetaketu also studied there. In the [_Chāndogya Upaniṣad_](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chandogya_Upanishad "Chandogya Upanishad"), Uddālaka Āruṇi himself favourably referred to Gāndhārī education to the [Vaideha](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Videha "Videha") king [Janaka](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Janaka "Janaka"). During the 6th century BCE, Gandhāra was an important imperial power in north-west Iron Age South Asia, with the [valley of Kaśmīra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kashmir_Valley "Kashmir Valley") being part of the kingdom. Due to this important position, Buddhist texts listed the Gandhāra kingdom as one of the sixteen _[_Mahājanapada_s](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mahajanapadas "Mahajanapadas")_ ("great realms") of Iron Age South Asia. It was the home of [Gandhari](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gandhari_\(Mahabharata\) "Gandhari (Mahabharata)"), the princess and her brother [Shakuni](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shakuni "Shakuni") the king of [Gandhara Kingdom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gandhara_Kingdom "Gandhara Kingdom").

### Pukkusāti and Achaemenid Gandhāra

![Xerxes I tomb, Gandāra soldier, c. 470 BCE](../media/gandhara/Xerxes_detail_Gandharan_enhanced.jpg)[Xerxes I](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Xerxes_I "Xerxes I") tomb, Gandāra soldier, c. 470 BCE

During the 6th or 5th century BCE, Gandhara was governed under the reign of King [Pukkusāti](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pushkarasarin "Pushkarasarin"). There are no historical facts known for certain about Pukkusāti, and all theories about his reign are speculative relying on later [Buddhist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Buddhism "Buddhism") sources. It is debated whether he ruled before or after the [Achaemenid conquest of the Indus Valley](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Achaemenid_conquest_of_the_Indus_Valley "Achaemenid conquest of the Indus Valley"), and is unknown what kind of relationship he historically had with the Persian Achaemenid rulers. According to [Buddhist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Buddhism "Buddhism") accounts written centuries later, he had forged diplomatic ties with [Magadha](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magadha_\(Mahajanapada\) "Magadha (Mahajanapada)") and achieved victories over neighbouring kingdoms such as that of the realm of [Avanti](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Avanti_\(region\) "Avanti (region)"). [Pukkusāti](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pushkarasarin "Pushkarasarin")'s kingdom was described as being 100 [Yojanas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yojana "Yojana") in width, approximately 500 to 800 miles wide, with his capital at [Taxila](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taxila "Taxila") in modern day [Punjab](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Punjab "Punjab") as stated in early [Jatakas.](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jataka_tales "Jataka tales")

It is noted by [R. C. Majumdar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/R._C._Majumdar "R. C. Majumdar") that Pukkusāti would have been contemporary to the [Achamenid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Achamenid "Achamenid") king [Cyrus the Great](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cyrus_the_Great "Cyrus the Great") and according to the scholar Buddha Prakash, Pukkusāti might have acted as a bulwark against the expansion of the [Persian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Persians "Persians") [Achaemenid Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Achaemenid_Empire "Achaemenid Empire") into Gandhara. This hypothesis posits that the army which [Nearchus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nearchus "Nearchus") claimed Cyrus had lost in [Gedrosia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gedrosia "Gedrosia") had been defeated by Pukkusāti's Gāndhārī kingdom. Therefore, following Prakash's position, the Achaemenids would have been able to conquer Gandhāra only after a period of decline after the reign of Pukkusāti, combined with the growth of Achaemenid power under the kings [Cambyses II](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cambyses_II "Cambyses II") and [Darius I](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Darius_the_Great "Darius the Great"). However, the presence of Gandhāra among the list of Achaemenid provinces in Darius's [Behistun Inscription](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Behistun_Inscription "Behistun Inscription") confirms that his empire had inherited this region from Cyrus. Assuming that Pukkusāti lived during the 6th century BCE, is unknown whether he remained in power after the Achaemenid conquest as a Persian vassal or if he was replaced by a Persian [satrap](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Satrap "Satrap"), although [Buddhist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Buddhism "Buddhism") sources claim that he renounced his throne and became a monk after becoming a disciple of the [Buddha](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gautama_Buddha "Gautama Buddha"). The annexation under Cyrus was limited to the Western sphere of Gandhāra as only during the reign of [Darius the Great](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Darius_the_Great "Darius the Great") did the region between the [Indus River](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indus_River "Indus River") and the [Jhelum River](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jhelum_River "Jhelum River") become annexed.

However, with [alternative chronologies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Buddha#Dating "The Buddha") which date the Buddha's lifetime (and his contemporary kings) as much as a century later, it is alternatively possible that [Pukkusāti](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pushkarasarin "Pushkarasarin") in fact lived as much as a century after the Achaemenid conquest. Among scholars who favour the latter chronology, it remains an open question for debate, what kind of relationship Pukkusāti historically had with the Persian Achaemenid rulers. Possible theories are: he "may belong to a period when the Achaemenids had already lost their hold over Indian provinces," or he may have been holding power in eastern parts of Gandhara such as [Taxila](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taxila "Taxila") (speculatively considered by some scholars to be outside the Achaemenid dominions), or may have been serving as a vassal of the Achaemenids but with autonomy to conduct warfare and diplomacy with independent Indian states, similar to the "active and often independent role the western satraps had [in Greek politics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greco-Persian_Wars "Greco-Persian Wars")". Thus it is considered that he may have been an important intermediary for [cultural influence between Ancient Persia and India](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Achaemenid_conquest_of_the_Indus_Valley#Influence_of_Achaemenid_culture_in_the_Indian_subcontinent "Achaemenid conquest of the Indus Valley").

[Megasthenes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Megasthenes "Megasthenes") [Indica](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indica_\(Megasthenes\) "Indica (Megasthenes)"), states that the [Achaemenids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Achaemenid_Empire "Achaemenid Empire") never conquered India and had only approached its borders after battling with the [Massagetae](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Massagetae "Massagetae"), it further states that the Persians summoned mercenaries specifically from the Oxydrakai tribe, who were previously known to have resisted the incursions of [Alexander the Great](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alexander_the_Great "Alexander the Great"), but they never entered their armies into the region of Gandhara.

![Athens coin (c. 500/490–485 BCE) discovered in Pushkalavati. This coin is the earliest known example of its type to be found so far east. Such coins were circulating in the area as currency, at least as far as the Indus, during the reign of the Achaemenids.](../media/gandhara/Athens_coin_discovered_in_Pushkalavati.jpg)[Athens](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Athens "Athens") coin (c. 500/490–485 BCE) discovered in [Pushkalavati](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pushkalavati "Pushkalavati"). This coin is the earliest known example of its type to be found so far east. Such coins were circulating in the area as currency, at least as far as the [Indus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indus "Indus"), during the reign of the [Achaemenids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Achaemenids "Achaemenids").

During the reign of [Xerxes I](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Xerxes_I "Xerxes I"), Gandharan troops were noted by [Herodotus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Herodotus "Herodotus") to have taken part in the [Second Persian invasion of Greece](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Second_Persian_invasion_of_Greece "Second Persian invasion of Greece") and were described as clothed similar to that of the [Bactrians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bactria "Bactria"). Herodotus states that during the battle they were led by the [Achamenid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Achamenid "Achamenid") general [Artyphius](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Artyphius "Artyphius").

Under Persian rule, a system of centralised administration, with a bureaucratic system, was introduced into the Indus Valley for the first time. Provinces or "satrapy" were established with provincial capitals. The [Gandhara](/source/gandhara/#Part_of_Achaemenid_Empire) satrapy, established 518 BCE with its capital at [Pushkalavati](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pushkalavati "Pushkalavati") ([Charsadda](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charsadda "Charsadda")). It was also during the [Achaemenid Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Achaemenid_Empire "Achaemenid Empire") rule of Gandhara that the [Kharosthi](/source/kharosthi/ "Kharosthi") script, the script of [Gandhari prakrit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gandhari_prakrit "Gandhari prakrit"), was born through the [Aramaic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aramaic "Aramaic") alphabet.

### Hellenistic era Gandhāra

According to [Arrian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arrian "Arrian")'s [Indica](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indica_\(Arrian\) "Indica (Arrian)"), the area corresponding to Gandhara situated between the [Kabul River](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kabul_River "Kabul River") and the [Indus River](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indus_River "Indus River") was inhabited by two tribes noted as the [Assakenoi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Assakenoi "Assakenoi") and Astakanoi whom he describes as 'Indian' and occupying the two great cities of [Massaga](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Massaga_\(ancient_city\) "Massaga (ancient city)") located around the [Swat valley](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Swat_valley "Swat valley") and [Pushkalavati](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pushkalavati "Pushkalavati") in modern day Peshawar.

The sovereign of [Taxila](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taxila "Taxila"), [Omphis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Omphis "Omphis"), formed an alliance with Alexander, motivated by a longstanding animosity towards [Porus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Porus "Porus"), who governed the region encompassed by the [Chenab](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chenab_River "Chenab River") and [Jhelum River](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jhelum_River "Jhelum River"). Omphis, in a gesture of goodwill, presented [Alexander the great](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alexander_the_Great "Alexander the Great") with significant gifts, esteemed among the Indian populace, and subsequently accompanied him on the expedition crossing the [Indus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indus_River "Indus River").

In 327 BCE, [Alexander the Great](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alexander_the_Great "Alexander the Great")'s military campaign progressed to Arigaum, situated in present-day [Nawagai](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nawagai,_Bajaur "Nawagai, Bajaur"), marking the initial encounter with the [Aspasians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aspasians "Aspasians"). [Arrian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arrian "Arrian") documented their implementation of a scorched earth strategy, evidenced by the city ablaze upon Alexander's arrival, with its inhabitants already fleeing. The [Aspasians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aspasians "Aspasians") fiercely contested Alexander's forces, resulting in their eventual defeat. Subsequently, Alexander traversed the River Guraeus in the contemporary [Dir District](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dir_\(region\) "Dir (region)"), engaging with the [Asvakas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Asvakas "Asvakas"), as chronicled in Sanskrit literature. The primary stronghold among the Asvakas, [Massaga](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Massaga_\(ancient_city\) "Massaga (ancient city)"), characterised as strongly fortified by [Quintus Curtius Rufus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quintus_Curtius_Rufus "Quintus Curtius Rufus"), became a focal point. Despite an initial standoff which led to Alexander being struck in the leg by an [Asvaka](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Asvaka "Asvaka") arrow, peace terms were negotiated between the Queen of Massaga and Alexander. However, when the defenders had vacated the fort, a fierce battle ensued when Alexander broke the treaty. According to [Diodorus Siculus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diodorus_Siculus "Diodorus Siculus"), the Asvakas, including women fighting alongside their husbands, valiantly resisted Alexander's army but were ultimately defeated.

### Mauryan Gandhāra

![Major Rock Edict of Ashoka in Mansehra](../media/gandhara/Upper_Boulder_with_Inscriptions_-_Mansehra_Rock_Edicts.jpg)[Major Rock Edict](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Major_Rock_Edicts "Major Rock Edicts") of Ashoka in [Mansehra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mansehra_District "Mansehra District")

During the [Mauryan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maurya_Empire "Maurya Empire") era, Gandhara held a pivotal position as a core territory within the empire, with [Taxila](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taxila "Taxila") serving as the provincial capital of the North West. [Chanakya](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chanakya "Chanakya"), a prominent figure in the establishment of the [Mauryan Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maurya_Empire "Maurya Empire"), played a key role by adopting [Chandragupta Maurya](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chandragupta_Maurya "Chandragupta Maurya"), the initial Mauryan emperor. Under Chanakya's tutelage, Chandragupta received a comprehensive education at Taxila, encompassing various arts of the time, including military training, for a duration spanning 7–8 years.

[Plutarch's](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plutarch "Plutarch") accounts suggest that [Alexander the Great](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alexander_the_Great "Alexander the Great") encountered a young [Chandragupta Maurya](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chandragupta_Maurya "Chandragupta Maurya") in the [Punjab](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Punjab "Punjab") region, possibly during his time at the university. Subsequent to Alexander's death, Chanakya and Chandragupta allied with [Trigarta](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trigarta_Kingdom "Trigarta Kingdom") king Parvataka to conquer the [Nanda Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nanda_Empire "Nanda Empire"). This alliance resulted in the formation of a composite army, comprising Gandharans and [Kambojas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kambojas "Kambojas"), as documented in the [Mudrarakshasa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mudrarakshasa "Mudrarakshasa").

[Bindusaras](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bindusara "Bindusara") reign witnessed a rebellion among the locals of [Taxila](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taxila "Taxila") to which according to the [Ashokavadana](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ashokavadana "Ashokavadana"), he dispatched [Ashoka](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ashoka "Ashoka") to quell the uprising. Upon entering the city, the populace conveyed that their rebellion was not against [Ashoka](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ashoka "Ashoka") or [Bindusara](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bindusara "Bindusara") but rather against oppressive ministers. In Ashoka's subsequent tenure as emperor, he appointed his son as the new governor of [Taxila](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taxila "Taxila"). During this time, Ashoka erected [numerous rock edicts](/source/edicts-of-ashoka/ "Edicts of Ashoka") in the region in the [Kharosthi](/source/kharosthi/ "Kharosthi") script and commissioned the construction of a monumental stupa in [Pushkalavati](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pushkalavati "Pushkalavati"), Western Gandhara, the location of which remains undiscovered to date.

According to the [Taranatha](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taranatha "Taranatha"), following the death of [Ashoka](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ashoka "Ashoka"), the northwestern region seceded from the [Maurya Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maurya_Empire "Maurya Empire"), and Virasena emerged as its king. Noteworthy for his diplomatic endeavours, Virasena's successor, [Subhagasena](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Subhagasena "Subhagasena"), maintained relations with the [Seleucid Greeks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Seleucid_Empire "Seleucid Empire"). This engagement is corroborated by [Polybius](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polybius "Polybius"), who records an instance where [Antiochus III the Great](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antiochus_III_the_Great "Antiochus III the Great") descended into India to renew his ties with King Subhagasena in 206 BCE, subsequently receiving a substantial gift of 150 elephants from the monarch.

### Indo-Greek Kingdom

![The founder of the Indo-Greek Kingdom Demetrius I (205–171 BCE), wearing the scalp of an elephant, symbol of his conquest of the Indus valley](../media/gandhara/Demetrius_I_of_Bactria.jpg)The founder of the [Indo-Greek Kingdom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indo-Greek_Kingdom "Indo-Greek Kingdom") [Demetrius I](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Demetrius_I_of_Bactria "Demetrius I of Bactria") (205–171 BCE), wearing the scalp of an elephant, symbol of his conquest of the Indus valley

The Indo-Greek king [Menander I](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Menander_I "Menander I") (reigned 155–130 BCE) drove the Greco-Bactrians out of Gandhara and beyond the [Hindu Kush](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hindu_Kush "Hindu Kush"), becoming king shortly after his victory.

His empire survived him in a fragmented manner until the last independent Greek king, [Strato II](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Strato_II "Strato II"), disappeared around 10 CE. Around 125 BCE, the Greco-Bactrian king [Heliocles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heliocles "Heliocles"), son of Eucratides, fled from the [Yuezhi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yuezhi "Yuezhi") invasion of Bactria and relocated to Gandhara, pushing the Indo-Greeks east of the [Jhelum River](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jhelum_River "Jhelum River"). The last known Indo-Greek ruler was [Theodamas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Theodamas "Theodamas"), from the [Bajaur](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bajaur "Bajaur") area of Gandhara, mentioned on a 1st-century CE signet ring, bearing the Kharoṣṭhī inscription _"Su Theodamasa"_ (_"Su"_ was the Greek transliteration of the [Kushan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kushan_Empire "Kushan Empire") royal title _"Shau"_ ("[Shah](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shah "Shah")" or "King")).

It is during this period that the fusion of Hellenistic and South Asian mythological, artistic and religious elements becomes most apparent, especially in the region of Gandhara.

Local Greek rulers still exercised a feeble and precarious power along the borderland, but the last vestige of the Greco-Indian rulers was finished by a people known to the old Chinese as the [Yueh-Chi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yuezhi "Yuezhi").

### Apracharajas

The Apracharajas were a historical dynasty situated in the region of Gandhara, extending from the governance of [Menander II](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Menander_II "Menander II") within the [Indo-Greek Kingdom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indo-Greek_Kingdom "Indo-Greek Kingdom") to the era of the early [Kushans](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kushans "Kushans"). Renowned for their significant support of [Buddhism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Buddhism "Buddhism"), this assertion is supported by swathes of discovered donations within their principal domain, between [Taxila](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taxila "Taxila") and [Bajaur](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bajaur_District "Bajaur District"). Archaeological evidence also establishes dynastic affiliations between them and the rulers of [Oddiyana](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oddiyana "Oddiyana") in modern-day [Swat.](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Swat_District "Swat District")

![Footprint of the Buddha. 1st century BCE, Gandhara.](../media/gandhara/Buddha-Footprint.jpeg)Footprint of the Buddha. 1st century BCE, Gandhara.

The dynasty is argued to have been founded by Vijayakamitra, identified as a vassal to [Menander II](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Menander_II "Menander II"), according to the [Shinkot casket](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shinkot_casket "Shinkot casket"). This epigraphic source further articulates that [King Vijayamitra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vijayamitra "Vijayamitra"), a descendant of Vijayakamitra, approximately half a century subsequent to the initial inscription, is credited with its restoration following inflicted damage. He is presumed to have gained the throne in c. 2 BCE after succeeding Visnuvarma, with a reign of three decades lasting til c. 32 CE before being succeeded by his son [Indravasu](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indravasu "Indravasu") and then further by Indravasu's grandson Indravarma II in c. 50 CE.

#### Indo-Scythian Kingdom

![One of the Buner reliefs showing Scythian soldiers dancing. Cleveland Museum of Art.](../media/gandhara/Buner_reliefs_Scythian_bacchanalian_cropped.jpg)One of the [Buner reliefs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Buner_reliefs "Buner reliefs") showing Scythian soldiers dancing. [Cleveland Museum of Art](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cleveland_Museum_of_Art "Cleveland Museum of Art").

The [Indo-Scythians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indo-Scythians "Indo-Scythians") were descended from the [Sakas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sakas "Sakas") (Scythians) who migrated from Central Asia into South Asia from the middle of the 2nd century BCE to the 1st century BCE. They displaced the Indo-Greeks and ruled a kingdom that stretched from Gandhara to [Mathura](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathura,_Uttar_Pradesh "Mathura, Uttar Pradesh"). The first Indo-Scythian king [Maues](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maues "Maues") established [Saka](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saka "Saka") hegemony by conquering [Indo-Greek](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indo-Greek "Indo-Greek") territories.

Some Aprachas are documented on the [Silver Reliquary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silver_Reliquary_of_Indravarman "Silver Reliquary of Indravarman") discovered at [Sirkap, near Taxila](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sirkap "Sirkap"), designating the title "Stratega," denoting a position equivalent to [Senapati](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Senapati "Senapati"), such as that of [Indravarma](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indravarma "Indravarma") who was a general during the reign of the Apracharaja [Vijayamitra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vijayamitra "Vijayamitra"). [Indravarma](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indravarma "Indravarma") is additionally noteworthy for receiving the above-mentioned [Silver Reliquary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silver_Reliquary_of_Indravarman "Silver Reliquary of Indravarman") from the [Indo-Scythian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indo-Scythians "Indo-Scythians") monarch [Kharahostes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kharahostes "Kharahostes"), which he subsequently re-dedicated as a [Buddhist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Buddhism "Buddhism") reliquary, indicating was a gift in exchange for tribute or assistance. According to another reliquary inscription Indravarma is noted as the Lord of Gandhara and general during the reign of Vijayamitra. According to Apracha chronology, [Indravarma](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indravarma "Indravarma") was the son of Visnuvarma, an Aprachraja preceding [Vijayamitra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vijayamitra "Vijayamitra").

[Indravarmas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indravarma "Indravarma") son [Aspavarma](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aspavarma "Aspavarma") is situated between 20 and 50 CE, during which numismatic evidence overlaps him with the [Indo-Scythian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indo-Scythians "Indo-Scythians") ruler [Azes II](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Azes_II "Azes II") and [Gondophares](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gondophares "Gondophares") of the [Indo-Parthians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indo-Parthians "Indo-Parthians") whilst also describing him as 'Stratega' or general of the Aprachas. In accordance with a Buddhist [Avadana](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Avadana "Avadana"), [Aspavarma](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aspavarma "Aspavarma") and a [Saka](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saka "Saka") noble, Jhadamitra, engaged in discussions concerning the establishment of accommodation for monks during the rainy seasons, displaying that he was a patron of [Buddhism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Buddhism "Buddhism"). A reliquary inscription dedicated to 50 CE, by a woman named Ariasrava, describes that her donation was made during the reign of [Gondophares](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gondophares "Gondophares") nephew, [Abdagases I](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abdagases_I "Abdagases I"), and [Aspavarma](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aspavarma "Aspavarma"), describing the joint rule by the Aprachas and the Indo-parthians.

#### Indo-Parthian Kingdom

![Ancient Buddhist monastery Takht-i-Bahi (a UNESCO World Heritage Site) constructed by the Indo-Parthians](../media/gandhara/Takht-e-bahi.jpg)Ancient [Buddhist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Buddhist "Buddhist") [monastery](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Monastery "Monastery") [Takht-i-Bahi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Takht-i-Bahi "Takht-i-Bahi") (a [UNESCO World Heritage Site](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/UNESCO_World_Heritage_Site "UNESCO World Heritage Site")) constructed by the Indo-Parthians

The [Indo-Parthian Kingdom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indo-Parthian_Kingdom "Indo-Parthian Kingdom") was ruled by the Gondopharid dynasty, named after its first ruler [Gondophares](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gondophares "Gondophares"). For most of their history, the leading Gondopharid kings held [Taxila](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taxila "Taxila") (in the present [Punjab](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Punjab,_Pakistan "Punjab, Pakistan") province of [Pakistan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pakistan "Pakistan")) as their residence, but during their last few years of existence, the capital shifted between [Kabul](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kabul "Kabul") and [Peshawar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peshawar "Peshawar"). These kings have traditionally been referred to as Indo-Parthians, as their coinage was often inspired by the [Arsacid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arsacid_dynasty_of_Parthia "Arsacid dynasty of Parthia") dynasty, but they probably belonged to wider groups of [Iranic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iranian_peoples "Iranian peoples") tribes who lived east of [Parthia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parthia "Parthia") proper, and there is no evidence that all the kings who assumed the title _Gondophares_, which means "Holder of Glory", were even related.

During the dominion of the [Indo-Parthians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indo-Parthian_Kingdom "Indo-Parthian Kingdom"), [Apracharaja](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apracharajas "Apracharajas") [Sasan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sases "Sases"), as described on numismatic evidence identifying him as the nephew of [Aspavarma](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aspavarma "Aspavarma"), emerged as a figure of significance. Aspavarman, a preceding Apracharaja contemporaneous with [Gondophares](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gondophares "Gondophares"), was succeeded by [Sasan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sases "Sases"), after having ascended from a subordinate governance role to a recognised position as one of Gondophares's successors. He assumed the position following [Abdagases I](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abdagases_I "Abdagases I"). The [Kushan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kushan_Empire "Kushan Empire") ruler [Vima Takto](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vima_Takto "Vima Takto") is known through numismatic evidence to have overstruck the coins of [Sasan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sases "Sases"), whilst a numismatic hoard had found coins of Sasan together with smaller coins of [Kujula Kadphises](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kujula_Kadphises "Kujula Kadphises") It has also been discovered that Sasan overstruck the coins of [Nahapana](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nahapana "Nahapana") of the [Western Satraps](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Western_Satraps "Western Satraps"), this line of coinage dating between 40 and 78 CE.

It was noted by [Philostratus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Philostratus "Philostratus") and [Apollonius of Tyana](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apollonius_of_Tyana "Apollonius of Tyana") upon their visit with [Phraotes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phraotes "Phraotes") in 46 AD, that during this time the Gandharans living between the [Kabul River](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kabul_River "Kabul River") and [Taxila](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taxila "Taxila") had coinage of [Orichalcum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orichalcum "Orichalcum") and Black brass, and their houses appearing as single-story structures from the outside, but upon entering, underground rooms were also present. They describe [Taxila](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taxila "Taxila") as being the same size as [Nineveh](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nineveh "Nineveh"), being walled like a Greek city whilst also being shaped with Narrow roads, and further describe [Phraotes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phraotes "Phraotes") kingdom as containing the old territory of [Porus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Porus "Porus"). Following an exchange with the king, [Phraotes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phraotes "Phraotes") is reported to have subsidised both barbarians and neighbouring states, to avert incursions into his kingdom. [Phraotes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phraotes "Phraotes") also recounts that his father, being the son of a king, had become an orphan from a young age. In accordance with Indian customs, two of his relatives assumed responsibility for his upbringing until they were killed by rebellious nobles during a ritualistic ceremony along the [Indus River](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indus_River "Indus River"). This event led to the usurpation of the throne, compelling Phraotes' father to seek refuge with the king situated beyond the [Hydaspes River](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydaspes_River "Hydaspes River"), in modern-day [Punjab](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Punjab "Punjab"), a ruler esteemed greater than Phraotes' father. Moreover, [Phraotes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phraotes "Phraotes") states that his father received an education facilitated by the [Brahmins](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brahmin "Brahmin") upon request to the king and married the daughter of the [Hydaspian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydaspes "Hydaspes") king, whilst having one son who was Phraotes himself. Phraotes proceeds to narrate the opportune moment he seized to reclaim his ancestral kingdom, sparked by a rebellion of the citizens of [Taxila](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taxila "Taxila") against the usurpers. With fervent support from the populace, Phraotes led a triumphant entry into the residence of the usurpers, whilst the citizens brandished torches, swords, and bows in a display of unified resistance.

#### Tribes mentioned by Pliny

During this period in the 1st century CE, [Pliny the Elder](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pliny_the_Elder "Pliny the Elder") notes a list of tribes in the [Vahika](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vahika "Vahika") and Gandhara regions spanning from the lower Indus to the mountain tribes near the [Hindu Kush](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hindu_Kush "Hindu Kush").

### Kushan Gandhāra

![Greco-Buddhist standing Buddha from Gandhara (1st–2nd century), Tokyo National Museum](../media/gandhara/Gandhara_Buddha_%28tnm%29.jpeg)[Greco-Buddhist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greco-Buddhism "Greco-Buddhism") [standing Buddha from Gandhara](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standing_Buddha_from_Gandhara_\(Tokyo\) "Standing Buddha from Gandhara (Tokyo)") (1st–2nd century), [Tokyo National Museum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tokyo_National_Museum "Tokyo National Museum")![Casket of Kanishka the Great, with Buddhist motifs](../media/gandhara/KanishkaCasket.JPG)[Casket of Kanishka the Great](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kanishka_casket "Kanishka casket"), with Buddhist motifs

The Kushans conquered [Bactria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bactria "Bactria") after having been defeated by the [Xiongnu](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Xiongnu "Xiongnu") and forced to retreat from the [Central Asian steppes.](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Central_Asian_steppe "Central Asian steppe") The [Yuezhi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yuezhi "Yuezhi") fragmented the region of Bactria into five distinct territories, with each tribe of the Yuezhi assuming dominion over a separate kingdom. However, a century after this division, [Kujula Kadphises](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kujula_Kadphises "Kujula Kadphises") of the Kushan tribe emerged victorious by destroying the other four [Yuezhi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yuezhi "Yuezhi") tribes and consolidating his reign as king. Kujula then invaded [Parthia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parthia "Parthia") and annexed the upper reaches of the [Kabul River](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kabul_River "Kabul River") before further conquering [Jibin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jibin "Jibin"). In 78 CE the [Indo-Parthians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indo-Parthian_Kingdom "Indo-Parthian Kingdom") seceded Gandhara to the Kushans with [Kujula Kadphises](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kujula_Kadphises "Kujula Kadphises") son [Vima Takto](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vima_Takto "Vima Takto") succeeding the [Apracharaja](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apracharajas "Apracharajas") [Sases](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sases "Sases") in [Taxila](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taxila "Taxila") and further conquering [Tianzhu (India)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tianzhu_\(India\) "Tianzhu (India)") before installing a general as a satrap.

According to the Xiyu Zhuan, the inhabitants residing in the upper reaches of the [Kabul River](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kabul_River "Kabul River") were extremely wealthy and excelled in commerce, with their cultural practices bearing resemblance to those observed in [Tianzhu (India)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tianzhu_\(India\) "Tianzhu (India)"). However, the text also characterises them as weak and easily conquered with their political allegiance never being constant. Over time, the region underwent successive annexations by [Tianzhu](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tianzhu_\(India\) "Tianzhu (India)"), [Jibin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jibin "Jibin"), and [Parthia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parthia "Parthia") during periods of their respective strength, only to be lost when these powers experienced a decline. The Xiyu Zhuan describes Tianzhu's customs as bearing similarities to that of the [Yuezhi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yuezhi "Yuezhi") and the inhabitants riding on elephants in warfare.

The Kushan period is considered the Golden Period of Gandhara. Peshawar Valley and Taxila are littered with ruins of _stupas_ and monasteries of this period. [Gandharan art](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greco-Buddhist_art "Greco-Buddhist art") flourished and produced some of the best pieces of sculpture from the Indian subcontinent. Gandhara's culture peaked during the reign of the great Kushan king [Kanishka the Great](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kanishka_the_Great "Kanishka the Great") (127 CE – 150 CE). The cities of Taxila (Takṣaśilā) at Sirsukh and Purushapura (modern-day [Peshawar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peshawar "Peshawar")) reached new heights. Purushapura along with [Mathura](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathura "Mathura") became the capital of the great empire stretching from Central Asia to Northern [India](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/India "India") with Gandhara being in the midst of it. Emperor [Kanishka](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kanishka "Kanishka") was a great patron of the Buddhist faith; [Buddhism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Buddhism "Buddhism") spread from [India](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/India "India") to [Central Asia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Central_Asia "Central Asia") and the Far East across Bactria and [Sogdia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sogdia "Sogdia"), where his empire met the [Han Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Han_dynasty "Han dynasty") of China. Buddhist art spread from Gandhara to other parts of Asia. In Gandhara, [Mahayana Buddhism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mahayana_Buddhism "Mahayana Buddhism") flourished and Buddha was represented in human form. Under the Kushans new Buddhist _stupas_ were built and old ones were enlarged. Huge statues of the Buddha were erected in monasteries and carved into the hillsides. Kanishka also built the 400-foot [Kanishka stupa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kanishka_Stupa "Kanishka Stupa") at Peshawar. This tower was reported by Chinese monks [Faxian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Faxian "Faxian"), [Song Yun](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Song_Yun "Song Yun"), and [Xuanzang](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Xuanzang "Xuanzang") who visited the country. The stupa was built during the Kushan era to house Buddhist relics and was among the tallest buildings in the ancient world.

### Kidarites

The [Kidarites](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kidarites "Kidarites") conquered [Peshawar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peshawar "Peshawar") and parts of the northwest Indian subcontinent including Gandhara probably sometime between 390 and 410 from Kushan empire, around the end of the rule of Gupta Emperor [Chandragupta II](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chandragupta_II "Chandragupta II") or beginning of the rule of [Kumaragupta I](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kumaragupta_I "Kumaragupta I"). It is probably the rise of the Hephthalites and the defeats against the Sasanians which pushed the Kidarites into northern India. Their last ruler in Gandhara was Kandik, c. 500 CE.

### Alchon Huns

Around 430 King [Khingila](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Khingila "Khingila"), the most notable [Alchon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alchon_Huns "Alchon Huns") ruler, emerged and took control of the routes across the [Hindu Kush](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hindu_Kush "Hindu Kush") from the Kidarites. Coins of the Alchons rulers Khingila and [Mehama](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mehama "Mehama") were found at the Buddhist monastery of [Mes Aynak](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mes_Aynak "Mes Aynak"), southeast of [Kabul](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kabul "Kabul"), confirming the Alchon presence in this area around 450–500 CE. The numismatic evidence as well as the so-called "[Hephthalite bowl](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hephthalite_silver_bowl "Hephthalite silver bowl")" from Gandhara, now in the [British Museum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/British_Museum "British Museum"), suggests a period of peaceful coexistence between the Kidarites and the Alchons, as it features two [Kidarite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kidarites "Kidarites") noble hunters, together with two Alchon hunters and one of the Alchons inside a medallion. At one point, the Kidarites withdrew from Gandhara, and the Alchons took over their mints from the time of [Khingila](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Khingila "Khingila").

The Alchons undertook the mass destruction of Buddhist monasteries and [stupas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stupas "Stupas") at [Taxila](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taxila "Taxila"), a high centre of learning, which never recovered from the destruction. Virtually all of the Alchon coins found in the area of Taxila were found in the ruins of burned down monasteries, where some of the invaders died alongside local defenders during the wave of destructions. It is thought that the [Kanishka stupa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kanishka_stupa "Kanishka stupa"), one of the most famous and tallest buildings in antiquity, was destroyed by them during their invasion of the area in the 460s CE. The [Mankiala stupa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mankiala_stupa "Mankiala stupa") was also vandalised during their invasions.

Mihirakula in particular is remembered by [Buddhist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Buddhist "Buddhist") sources to have been a "terrible persecutor of their religion" in Gandhara. During the reign of [Mihirakula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mihirakula "Mihirakula"), over one thousand Buddhist monasteries throughout Gandhara are said to have been destroyed. In particular, the writings of Chinese monk [Xuanzang](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Xuanzang "Xuanzang") from 630 CE explained that Mihirakula ordered the destruction of Buddhism and the expulsion of monks. The Buddhist art of Gandhara, in particular [Greco-Buddhist art](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greco-Buddhist_art "Greco-Buddhist art"), became extinct around this period. When Xuanzang visited Gandhara in c. 630 CE, he reported that Buddhism had drastically declined in favour of [Shaivism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shaivism "Shaivism") and that most of the monasteries were deserted and left in ruins. It is also noted by [Kalhana](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kalhana "Kalhana") that [Brahmins](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brahmin "Brahmin") of Gandhara accepted from [Mihirakula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mihirakula "Mihirakula") gifts of [Agraharams](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Agraharam "Agraharam"). [Kalhana](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kalhana "Kalhana") also noted in his [Rajatarangini](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rajatarangini "Rajatarangini") how Mihirakula oppressed local [Brahmins](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brahmin "Brahmin") of South Asia and imported Gandharan Brahmins into [Kashmir](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kashmir "Kashmir") and India and states that he had given thousands of villages to these Brahmins in Kashmir.

### Turk and Hindu Shahis

![Horseman on a coin of Spalapati, i.e. the \"War-lord\" of the Shahis. The headgear has been interpreted as a turban.](../media/gandhara/Horseman_on_Hindu_Shahi_coinage.jpg)Horseman on a coin of Spalapati, i.e. the "War-lord" of the [Shahis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hindu_Shahis "Hindu Shahis"). The headgear has been interpreted as a [turban](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Turban "Turban").

The [Turk Shahis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Turk_Shahis "Turk Shahis") ruled Gandhara until 843 CE when they were overthrown by the [Hindu Shahis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hindu_Shahis "Hindu Shahis"). The Hindu Shahis are believed to belong to the Uḍi/Oḍi tribe, namely the people of [Oddiyana](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oddiyana "Oddiyana") in Gandhara.

The history of the Hindu Shahis begins in 843 CE with Kallar deposing the last [Turk Shahi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Turk_Shahis "Turk Shahis") ruler, Lagaturman. Samanta succeeded him, and it was during his reign that the region of [Kabul](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kabul "Kabul") was lost to the [Persianate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Persianate "Persianate") [Saffarid empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saffarid_empire "Saffarid empire"). Lalliya replaced Samanta soon after and re-conquered Kabul whilst also subduing the region of [Zabulistan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zabulistan "Zabulistan"). He is additionally noteworthy for coming into conflict with [Samkaravarman](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sankaravarman "Sankaravarman") of the [Utpala dynasty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Utpala_dynasty "Utpala dynasty"), resulting in his victory and the latter's death in [Hazara](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hazara_region "Hazara region") and was the first Shahi noted by [Kalhana](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kalhana "Kalhana"). He is depicted as a great ruler with strength to the standard where kings of other regions would seek shelter in his capital of [Udabhanda](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Udabhanda "Udabhanda"), a change from the previous capital of [Kabul](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kabul "Kabul"). Bhimadeva, the next most notable ruler, is most significant for vanquishing the [Samanid Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Samanid_Empire "Samanid Empire") in Ghazni and Kabul in response to their conquests, his grand-daughter [Didda](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Didda "Didda") was also the last ruler of the [Lohara dynasty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lohara_dynasty "Lohara dynasty"). Jayapala then gained control and was brought into conflict with the newly formed [Ghaznavid Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ghaznavid_Empire "Ghaznavid Empire"), however, he was eventually defeated. During his rule and that of his son and successor, Anandapala, the kingdom of [Lahore](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lahore "Lahore") was conquered. The following Shahi rulers all resisted the Ghaznavids but were ultimately unsuccessful, resulting in the downfall of the empire in 1026 CE.

### Rediscovery

![Many stupas, such as the Shingerdar stupa in Ghalegay, are scattered throughout the region near Peshawar.](../media/gandhara/Shingerdar_Stupa1.jpg)Many stupas, such as the Shingerdar stupa in [Ghalegay](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ghalegay "Ghalegay"), are scattered throughout the region near [Peshawar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peshawar "Peshawar").

By the time Gandhara had been absorbed into the empire of Mahmud of Ghazni, Buddhist buildings were already in ruins and Gandhara's art had been forgotten. After Al-Biruni, the Kashmiri writer Kalhaṇa wrote his book _Rajatarangini_ in 1151. He recorded some events that took place in Gandhara and provided details about its last royal dynasty and capital [Udabhandapura](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hund,_Khyber_Pakhtunkhwa "Hund, Khyber Pakhtunkhwa").

In the 19th century, British soldiers and administrators started taking an interest in the ancient history of the Indian Subcontinent. In the 1830s coins of the post-Ashoka period were discovered, and in the same period, Chinese travelogues were translated. [Charles Masson](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charles_Masson "Charles Masson"), [James Prinsep](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/James_Prinsep "James Prinsep"), and [Alexander Cunningham](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alexander_Cunningham "Alexander Cunningham") deciphered the [Kharosthi](/source/kharosthi/ "Kharosthi") script in 1838. Chinese records provided locations and site plans for Buddhist shrines. Along with the discovery of coins, these records provided clues necessary to piece together the history of Gandhara. In 1848 Cunningham found Gandhara sculptures north of Peshawar. He also identified the site of Taxila in the 1860s. From then on a large number of Buddhist statues were discovered in the Peshawar valley.

[Archaeologist John Marshall](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Marshall_\(archaeologist\) "John Marshall (archaeologist)") excavated at Taxila between 1912 and 1934. He discovered separate Greek, Parthian, and Kushan cities and a large number of _stupas_ and monasteries. These discoveries helped to piece together much more of the chronology of the history of Gandhara and its art.

After 1947 [Ahmed Hassan Dani](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ahmed_Hassan_Dani "Ahmed Hassan Dani") and the Archaeology Department at the [University of Peshawar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/University_of_Peshawar "University of Peshawar") made several discoveries in the Peshawar and Swat Valley. Excavation of many of the sites of the Gandhara Civilization is being done by researchers from Peshawar and several universities around the world.

## Culture

### Language

Gandhara's language was a [Prakrit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prakrit "Prakrit") or "[Middle Indo-Aryan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Middle_Indo-Aryan_languages "Middle Indo-Aryan languages")" dialect, usually called [Gāndhārī](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gandhari_language "Gandhari language"). Under the [Kushan Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kushan_Empire "Kushan Empire"), Gāndhārī spread into adjoining regions of South and Central Asia. It used the [Kharosthi](/source/kharosthi/ "Kharosthi") script, which is derived from the [Aramaic script](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aramaic_alphabet "Aramaic alphabet"), and it died out about in the 4th century CE.

Linguistic evidence links some groups of the [Dardic languages](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dardic_languages "Dardic languages") with Gandhari. The [Kohistani languages](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kohistani_languages "Kohistani languages"), now all being displaced from their original homelands, were once more widespread in the region and most likely descend from the ancient dialects of the region of Gandhara. The last to disappear was [Tirahi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tirahi_language "Tirahi language"), still spoken some years ago in a few villages in the vicinity of [Jalalabad](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jalalabad "Jalalabad") in eastern Afghanistan, by descendants of migrants expelled from [Tirah](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tirah "Tirah") by the [Afridi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Afridi "Afridi") [Pashtuns](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pashtuns "Pashtuns") in the 19th century. [Georg Morgenstierne](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Georg_Morgenstierne "Georg Morgenstierne") claimed that Tirahi is "probably the remnant of a dialect group extending from Tirah through the [Peshawar district](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peshawar_District "Peshawar District") into [Swat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Swat_District "Swat District") and [Dir](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dir_\(region\) "Dir (region)")". Nowadays, it must be entirely extinct and the region is now dominated by [Iranian languages](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iranian_languages "Iranian languages") brought in by later [immigrations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Settler "Settler"), such as [Pashto](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pashto "Pashto"). Among the modern day Indo-Aryan languages still spoken today, [Torwali](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Torwali_language "Torwali language") shows the closest linguistic affinity possible to _Niya_, a dialect of Gāndhārī.

#### Mahāyāna Buddhism

As per Pali sources, Buddhism first reached Gandhara following the [Third Buddhist council](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Third_Buddhist_council "Third Buddhist council") which was held in [Pataliputra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pataliputra "Pataliputra") during the reign of [Ashoka](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ashoka "Ashoka") in the third-century BCE. Various monks were dispatched to different parts of the empire and the missionary dispatched to Gandhara specifically was [Majjhantika](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Majjhantika "Majjhantika") who originated from the city of [Varanasi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Varanasi "Varanasi") in [India](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/India "India").

Mahāyāna [Pure Land sutras](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Buddhist_sutras#Pure_Land_Sutras "Buddhist sutras") were brought from the Gandhāra region to China as early as 147 CE, when the [Kushan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kushan_Empire "Kushan Empire") monk [Lokakṣema](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lokaksema_\(Buddhist_monk\) "Lokaksema (Buddhist monk)") began translating some of the first Buddhist sutras into Chinese. The earliest of these translations show evidence of having been translated from the Gāndhārī language. Lokakṣema translated important [Mahāyāna sūtras](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mahayana_sutras "Mahayana sutras") such as the _[Aṣṭasāhasrikā Prajñāpāramitā Sūtra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prajñāpāramitā "Prajñāpāramitā")_, as well as rare, early Mahāyāna sūtras on topics such as [samādhi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Samādhi_\(Buddhism\) "Samādhi (Buddhism)"), and meditation on the Buddha [Akṣobhya](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Akshobhya "Akshobhya"). Lokaksema's translations continue to provide insight into the early period of Mahāyāna Buddhism. This corpus of texts often includes and emphasises ascetic practices forest dwelling, and absorption in states of meditative concentration:

> Paul Harrison has worked on some of the texts that are arguably the earliest versions we have of the Mahāyāna sūtras, those translated into Chinese in the last half of the second century AD by the Indo-Scythian translator Lokakṣema. Harrison points to the enthusiasm in the Lokakṣema sūtra corpus for the extra ascetic practices, for dwelling in the forest, and above all for states of meditative absorption (_samādhi_). Meditation and meditative states seem to have occupied a central place in early Mahāyāna, certainly because of their spiritual efficacy but also because they may have given access to fresh revelations and inspiration.

Some scholars believe that the Mahāyāna _[Longer Sukhāvatīvyūha Sūtra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Infinite_Life_Sutra "Infinite Life Sutra")_ was compiled in the age of the Kushan Empire in the 1st and 2nd centuries CE, by order of [Mahīśāsaka](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mahisasaka "Mahisasaka") [bhikṣus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bhikkhu "Bhikkhu") which flourished in the Gandhāra region. However, it is likely that the longer _Sukhāvatīvyūha_ owes greatly to the [Mahāsāṃghika](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mahāsāṃghika "Mahāsāṃghika")-[Lokottaravāda](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lokottaravāda "Lokottaravāda") sect as well for its compilation, and in this sutra, there are many elements in common with the Lokottaravādin _[Mahāvastu](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mahavastu "Mahavastu")_. There are also images of [Amitābha](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Amitābha "Amitābha") Buddha with the [bodhisattvas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bodhisattva "Bodhisattva") [Avalokiteśvara](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Avalokiteśvara "Avalokiteśvara") and [Mahāsthāmaprāpta](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mahāsthāmaprāpta "Mahāsthāmaprāpta") which were made in Gandhāra during the Kushan era.

The _Mañjuśrīmūlakalpa_ records that Kaniṣka of the Kushan Empire presided over the establishment of the Mahāyāna [Prajñāpāramitā](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prajnaparamita "Prajnaparamita") teachings in the northwest. [Tāranātha](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taranatha "Taranatha") wrote that in this region, 500 [bodhisattvas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bodhisattva "Bodhisattva") attended the council at Jālandhra monastery during the time of Kaniṣka, suggesting some institutional strength for Mahāyāna in the north-west during this period. [Edward Conze](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Edward_Conze "Edward Conze") goes further to say that Prajñāpāramitā had great success in the north-west during the Kushan period, and may have been the "fortress and hearth" of early Mahāyāna, but not its origin, which he associates with the Mahāsāṃghika branch of Buddhism.

#### Other religions

[Zoroastrianism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zoroastrianism "Zoroastrianism") also was present in Gandhara since the Persian conquest, likely continuing to the reign of Ashoka and subsequently: some scholars have speculated that its presence may have influenced the development of unique aspects of Mahayana Buddhism in Gandhara.

Gandhara, especially Taxila, during Kushan times also played an influential role in the development of arts and culture of the [Hindu synthesis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_Hinduism#Hindu_synthesis_and_Classical_Hinduism_\(c._200_BCE_–_1200_CE\) "History of Hinduism"), as the region's artistic traditions were influential to some of the early depictions of deities in [Hindu art](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hindu_art#Hindu_art_under_the_Kushans_\(2nd-3rd_century_CE\) "Hindu art").

### Art

![Lid with seated male figure, Gandhara. (1st–2nd century)](../media/gandhara/Lid_with_seated_male_figure%2C_Gandhara%2C_Pakistan.jpeg)Lid with seated male figure, Gandhara. (1st–2nd century)

Gandhāra is noted for the distinctive **Gandhāra style** of [Buddhist art](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Buddhist_art "Buddhist art"), which shows the influence of [Hellenistic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hellenistic_art "Hellenistic art") and Eastern Indo-Aryan influences from the [Gangetic Valley](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indo-Gangetic_Plain "Indo-Gangetic Plain"). The [Gandhāran art](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greco-Buddhist_art "Greco-Buddhist art") flourished and achieved its peak during the [Kushan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kushan_Empire "Kushan Empire") period, from the 1st to the 5th centuries, but it declined and was destroyed after the invasion of the [Alchon Huns](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alchon_Huns "Alchon Huns") in the 5th century.

Siddhārtha shown as a bejewelled prince (before [Siddhārtha renounces palace life](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Great_Renunciation "Great Renunciation")) is a common motif. [Stucco](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stucco "Stucco"), as well as stone, were widely used by sculptors in Gandhara for the decoration of monastic and cult buildings. Buddhist imagery combined with some artistic elements from the cultures of the Hellenistic world. An example is the youthful Buddha, his hair in wavy curls, similar to statutes of [Apollo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apollo "Apollo"). Sacred artworks and architectural decorations used limestone for stucco composed by a mixture of local crushed rocks (i.e. [schist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Schist "Schist") and [granite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Granite "Granite")) which resulted compatible with the outcrops located in the mountains northwest of [Islamabad](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Islamabad "Islamabad").

The artistic traditions of Gandhara art can be divided into the following phases:

*   [Indo-Greek art](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indo-Greek_art "Indo-Greek art"); 2nd century BCE to 1st century CE
*   [Indo-Scythian art](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indo-Scythian_art "Indo-Scythian art"); 1st century BCE to 1st century CE
*   [Kushan art](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kushan_art "Kushan art"); 1st century CE to 4th century CE
