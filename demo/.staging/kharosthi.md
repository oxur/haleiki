**Kharosthi script** ([Gāndhārī](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gāndhārī_language "Gāndhārī language"): 𐨑𐨪𐨆𐨮𐨿𐨛𐨁𐨌 𐨫𐨁𐨤𐨁, romanized: _kharoṣṭhī lipi_), also known as the **Gandhari script** (𐨒𐨌𐨣𐨿𐨢𐨌𐨪𐨁𐨌 𐨫𐨁𐨤𐨁, _gāndhārī lipi_), was an ancient Indic script originally developed in the [Gandhara Region](/source/gandhara/ "Gandhara") of the north-western [Indian subcontinent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indian_subcontinent "Indian subcontinent"), between the 5th and 3rd century BCE. Primarily used by the people of [Gandhara](/source/gandhara/ "Gandhara") in various parts of [South Asia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/South_Asia "South Asia") and [Central Asia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Central_Asia "Central Asia"), Kharosthi remained in use until it died out in its homeland around the 5th century CE. It was also in use in [Bactria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bactria "Bactria"), the [Kushan Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kushan_Empire "Kushan Empire"), [Sogdia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sogdia "Sogdia"), and along the [Silk Road](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silk_Road "Silk Road"). There is some evidence it may have survived until the [7th century](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/7th_century "7th century") in [Khotan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Khotan "Khotan") and [Niya](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Niya_\(Tarim_Basin\) "Niya (Tarim Basin)"), both cities in [Tarim Basin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tarim_Basin "Tarim Basin").

## History

![Routes of ancient scripts of the subcontinent traveling to other parts of Asia (Kharosthi shown in blue)](../media/kharosthi/Brahmic_script_travel_from_India.png)Routes of ancient scripts of the subcontinent traveling to other parts of Asia (Kharosthi shown in blue)

The name Kharosthi may derive from the Hebrew _kharosheth_, a Semitic word for writing, or from [Old Iranian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Old_Iranian "Old Iranian") \*_xšaθra-pištra_, which means "royal writing". The script was earlier also known as _Indo-Bactrian script_, _Kabul script_ and _Arian-Pali_.

Scholars are not in agreement as to whether the Kharosthi script evolved gradually, or was the deliberate work of a single inventor. An analysis of the script forms shows a clear dependency on the [Aramaic alphabet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aramaic_alphabet "Aramaic alphabet") but with extensive modifications. Kharosthi seems to be derived from a form of Aramaic, which was used in administrative work during the reign of [Darius the Great](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Darius_the_Great "Darius the Great") (in contrast with the monumental [Old Persian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Old_Persian "Old Persian") [cuneiform](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cuneiform "Cuneiform") used for public inscriptions). One theory suggests that the Aramaic script arrived with the [Achaemenid conquest of the Indus Valley](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Achaemenid_conquest_of_the_Indus_Valley "Achaemenid conquest of the Indus Valley") in 500 BCE and evolved over the next 200+ years to reach its final form by the 3rd century BCE where it appears in some of the Edicts of Ashoka. However, no intermediate forms have yet been found to confirm this evolutionary model, and rock and coin inscriptions from the 3rd century BCE onward show a unified and standard form. An inscription in Aramaic dating back to the 4th century BCE was found in [Sirkap](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sirkap "Sirkap"), testifying to the presence of the Aramaic script in present-day Pakistan. According to [Sir John Marshall](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Marshall_\(archaeologist\) "John Marshall (archaeologist)"), this seems to confirm that Kharoshthi was later developed from Aramaic.

While the Brahmi script remained in use for centuries, Kharosthi seems to have been abandoned after the 2nd–3rd century AD. Because of the substantial differences between the Semitic-derived Kharosthi script and its successors, knowledge of Kharosthi may have declined rapidly once the script was supplanted by Brahmi-derived scripts, until its re-discovery by Western scholars in the 19th century.

The Kharosthi script was deciphered separately almost concomitantly by [James Prinsep](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/James_Prinsep "James Prinsep") (in 1835, published in the _Journal of the Asiatic society of Bengal_, India) and by [Carl Ludwig Grotefend](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carl_Ludwig_Grotefend "Carl Ludwig Grotefend") (in 1836, published in _Blätter für Münzkunde_, Germany), with Grotefend "evidently not aware" of Prinsep's article, followed by [Christian Lassen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christian_Lassen "Christian Lassen") (1838). They all used the bilingual coins of the [Indo-Greek Kingdom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indo-Greek_Kingdom "Indo-Greek Kingdom") (obverse in Greek, reverse in [Pali](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pali "Pali"), using the Kharosthi script). This in turn led to the reading of the [Edicts of Ashoka](/source/edicts-of-ashoka/ "Edicts of Ashoka"), some of which were written in the Kharosthi script (the [Major Rock Edicts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Major_Rock_Edicts "Major Rock Edicts") at [Mansehra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mansehra "Mansehra") and [Shahbazgarhi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shahbazgarhi "Shahbazgarhi")).

The study of the Kharosthi script was recently invigorated by the discovery of the [Gandhāran Buddhist texts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gandhāran_Buddhist_texts "Gandhāran Buddhist texts"), a set of [birch bark manuscripts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Birch_bark_manuscript "Birch bark manuscript") written in Kharosthi, discovered near the Afghan city of [Hadda](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hadda,_Afghanistan "Hadda, Afghanistan") just west of the [Khyber Pass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Khyber_Pass "Khyber Pass") in [Pakistan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pakistan "Pakistan"). The manuscripts were donated to the [British Library](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/British_Library "British Library") in 1994. The entire set of British Library manuscripts are dated to the 1st century CE, although other collections from different institutions contain Kharosthi manuscripts from 1st century BCE to 3rd century CE, making them the oldest Buddhist manuscripts yet discovered.

## Alphabet

![The words inscription of the dharma (Prakrit: 𐨢𐨌𐨨𐨡𐨁𐨤𐨁, romanized: dhrāma dipu) in Edict No. 1 of the Major Rock Edict of Ashoka (circa 250 BCE).](../media/kharosthi/Dhrama_Dipi_inscription_in_the_Shahbazgarhi_First_Edict_in_the_Kharosthi_script.jpg)The words _inscription of the [dharma](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dharma "Dharma")_ ([Prakrit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prakrit "Prakrit"): 𐨢𐨌𐨨𐨡𐨁𐨤𐨁, romanized: _dhrāma dipu_) in Edict No. 1 of the [Major Rock Edict](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Major_Rock_Edict "Major Rock Edict") of [Ashoka](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ashoka "Ashoka") (circa 250 BCE).

Kharosthi is mostly written right to left. Some variations in both the number and order of syllables occur in extant texts.

The Kharosthi alphabet is also known as the arapacana alphabet, and follows the order.

: a ra pa ca na : la da ba ḍa ṣa : va ta ya ṣṭa : ka sa ma ga stha : ja śva dha śa kha : kṣa sta jñā rtha (or ha) : bha cha sma hva tsa : gha ṭha ṇa pha ska : ysa śca ṭa ḍha

This alphabet was used in [Gandharan Buddhism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gandharan_Buddhism "Gandharan Buddhism") as a mnemonic for the [Pañcaviṃśatisāhasrikā Prajñāpāramitā Sūtra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Large_Prajñāpāramitā_Sūtras#Pañcaviṃśatisāhasrikā_Prajñāpāramitā_Sūtra "Large Prajñāpāramitā Sūtras"), a series of verses on the nature of phenomena; this is analogous to the [Shiva Sutras](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shiva_Sutras "Shiva Sutras") of Brahmic scripts. In modern Himalayan and East Asian Buddhism, the first line _arapacana_ is preserved in the [mantra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mantra "Mantra") of [Manjushri](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Manjushri "Manjushri"), the Bodhisattva of transcendent wisdom.

A bar above a consonant ⟨𐨸⟩ can be used to indicate various modified pronunciations depending on the consonant, such as nasalization or aspiration. It is used with k, ṣ, g, c, j, n, m, ś, ṣ, s, and h.

The _cauda_ ⟨𐨹⟩ changes how consonants are pronounced in various ways, particularly [fricativization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fricative_consonant "Fricative consonant"). It is used with g, j, ḍ, t, d, p, y, v, ś, and s.

The dot below ⟨𐨺⟩ is used with m and h, but its precise phonetic function is unknown.

### Vowels and syllables

![Kharoshthi on a coin of Indo-Greek king Artemidoros Aniketos, reading Maues, King of kings and son of Artemidorus, (Gāndhārī: 𐨪𐨗𐨟𐨁𐨪𐨗𐨯𐨨𐨆𐨀𐨯𐨤𐨂𐨟𐨯𐨕𐨪𐨿𐨟𐨅𐨨𐨁𐨡𐨆𐨪𐨯, romanized: Rajatirajasa Moasa putasa cha Artemidorasa).](../media/kharosthi/Artemidoros_coin_obverse_with_transliteration.jpg)Kharoshthi on a coin of [Indo-Greek](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indo-Greek "Indo-Greek") king [Artemidoros Aniketos](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Artemidoros_Aniketos "Artemidoros Aniketos"), reading _[Maues](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maues "Maues"), King of kings and son of [Artemidorus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Artemidoros_Aniketos "Artemidoros Aniketos")_, ([Gāndhārī](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gāndhārī_language "Gāndhārī language"): 𐨪𐨗𐨟𐨁𐨪𐨗𐨯𐨨𐨆𐨀𐨯𐨤𐨂𐨟𐨯𐨕𐨪𐨿𐨟𐨅𐨨𐨁𐨡𐨆𐨪𐨯, romanized: _Rajatirajasa Moasa putasa cha Artemidorasa_).

Kharosthi includes only one standalone vowel character, which is used for initial vowels in words. Other initial vowels use the _a_ character modified by diacritics. Each syllable includes the short /a/ sound by default, with other vowels being indicated by diacritic marks.

Long vowels are marked with the diacritic ⟨𐨌⟩. An [anusvara](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anusvara "Anusvara") ⟨𐨎⟩ indicates [nasalization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nasalization "Nasalization") of the vowel or a nasal segment following the vowel. A [visarga](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Visarga "Visarga") ⟨𐨏⟩ indicates the unvoiced syllable-final /h/. It can also be used as a vowel length marker. A further diacritic, the _double ring below_ ⟨𐨍⟩ appears with vowels -a and -u in some Central Asian documents, but its precise phonetic function is unknown.

Salomon has established that the vowel order is /a e i o u/, akin to Semitic scripts, rather than the usual vowel order for Indic scripts /a i u e o/.

## Numerals

Kharosthi included a set of numerals that are reminiscent of [Roman numerals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roman_numerals "Roman numerals") and [Psalter Pahlavi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Psalter_Pahlavi "Psalter Pahlavi") Numerals. The system is based on an additive and a multiplicative principle, but does not have the subtractive feature used in the Roman numeral system.
